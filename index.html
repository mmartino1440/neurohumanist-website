The Journey Thus Far

My path to medicine and neuroscience has been full of unexpected serendipity and good fortune, one which has required faith in my vision for the future and patience, among other qualities. Now, almost 10 years after I first began to explore the fascinating world of biomedical research, my current position seems obvious, or at the very least logically consistent with my recollections of that time. Yet I know for certain that my younger selves would be incredulous upon learning what they'll grow up to become. How did I arrive at this point, one that I never considered yet is paradoxically obvious when considered in hindsight?

My Philosophy: An Unfolding Journey

This journey was, and continues to be, fueled by an inquisitive nature I didn't always recognize in myself. It was through the encouragement of teachers and mentors over many years that I began to see the potential of my own curiosity. Then, during my final undergraduate semester in a class on the biology of cardiovascular disease, something unexpected happened. Learning the intricate details of cellular signaling pathways struck me with a profound sense of awe—that we could understand life at this resolution was a revelation. Around the same time, working with a client sparked an idea for an intervention aimed at reducing adverse healthcare events by educating patients' families to encourage multi-generational lifestyle shifts.

Though this specific project never came to be, it introduced me to the potential for truly impactful work and led me to conclude: if I had ideas that could help others, I needed to learn how to ask those questions and perform those studies safely, ethically, and with precision. This dual motivation—the awe of basic science and the drive for impactful application—led me to a master's degree, where I became enthralled with every facet of science. I devoured philosophy, the biographies of great thinkers, and even mathematics—a subject I had historically abhorred. As I entered medical school, an itch to explore the brain persisted. When I was finally exposed to the nervous system, I knew I had to commit. It was a risk; I could have fallen back on metabolism research, but I couldn't pass up the chance to discover if neuroscience and psychiatry were where I could finally unite my eclectic interests in pursuit of a higher objective.

This website is a reflection of my journey: a commitment to a science that is not isolated, but is in constant, dynamic conversation with the full breadth of human knowledge. Here, I share my philosophical perspectives, insights from medical and graduate school, interesting science, and other musings I feel would be of value to others.

Core Research: Systems Neuroscience of Fear & Anxiety

As a basic systems neuroscientist in pre-clinical training, my primary work aims to understand how experiences of intense fear become sensitized in the brain. I investigate the whole-brain signature of fear sensitization at both regional and network scales. By combining cutting-edge neuroimaging techniques (like whole-brain c-Fos mapping with iDISCO and light sheet microscopy) with targeted chemogenetic manipulations in my behavioral paradigms, I seek to identify the distributed circuits that mediate the transition from adaptive fear to maladaptive, generalized anxiety.

Prompt Engineering for Scientists

Something from Nothing

8 min read

As we have learned from quantum mechanics, relying on our intuition about how things work from our limited human perspective greatly limits our ability to understand nature...

The laws that govern the fundamental particles that are the building blocks of our universe are extremely unintuitive and defy any sort of common sense or intuition we may have, which is largely explained by our historical lack of observational evidence of these processes. Only within the last century have we come to gain an appreciation for the atomic and subatomic foundations which our observable world is built upon.

These paradigm shifts in our understanding of nature have largely been the result of great abstractions and departures from convention. The minds which first stumbled upon and shared such insights generally faced a great deal of opposition and criticism for years before seeing their hypotheses come to be accepted after rigorous testing and validation of their findings, if they were so fortunate. To consider how something comes from nothing requires a departure from convention and what we generally assume to be "common sense".

First, we must define nothing: nothingness describes a vacuum, in which no matter exists. Without matter, time and space also cease to have meaning (in other words, they do not exist). In a reality where time has no meaning is one where nothing is impossible, because for all we know nothing is truly impossible but simply highly improbable (so exceedingly improbable that when expressed as the amount of time needed for such an event to occur, we can say it is in principle impossible), improbable things can occur, including the emergence of something from nothing.

From Dogma to Discernment: A Spiritual and Philosophical Journey

My journey with faith began in a place of earnest devotion. As a child, I found solace in the simple, powerful stories of my Christian upbringing. I was drawn to the sense of community and the clear moral compass it provided. I was a true believer, and I saw my faith as the single source of truth in a chaotic world.

However, as I grew older and my mind began to deepen, I became increasingly disillusioned. I found the narratives of a personal, interventionist God to be inconsistent with the scientific understanding of the world I was so passionately pursuing. This led me to a period of profound suspicion, and even hostility, toward organized religion. I saw the stories not as a source of wisdom, but as a tool used to rationalize a worldview that I felt was small, rigid, and often lacking in compassion.

But the human need for stories, for a framework to understand our world, is a powerful and very real thing. I have come to realize that the fault is not with the stories themselves, but with how they are wielded. The stories that we tell ourselves are an integral part of the human experience, and they can be used to rationalize actions that either violate or champion humanitarian ideals.

This journey has led me to a new place, a place of spiritual and philosophical liberation. I now understand God not as a personal being, but as a metaphor to describe the natural laws and rules that constrain, and yet give rise to, the reality we inhabit. My spirituality places the individual above the material, and my beliefs are a complex tapestry woven from a lifelong search for truth.

The Foundation of a New Philosophy

The conversation I recently had with a loved one illustrates the very core of this philosophy.

When he told me that I was subscribing to the beliefs of the "far left wing media and politicians that promote division and hate," I did not hear a person trying to engage with my views. I heard someone operating from a simple, tribal paradigm of us versus them. This is not a judgment on him, but a way to understand his worldview.

My worldview is a much more complex and compassionate one. I believe that all human life has equal worth, and all human suffering is worthy of sympathy. I believe that the human mind is a complex and fragile thing, and that our actions are not always within our control.

My purpose in life is to use my training as a psychiatric-neuroscientist to lobby for the widespread adoption of humanitarian ideals. The core of my philosophy, the culmination of a decade of scientific inquiry and a lifetime of curiously pursuing answers to the many questions I have, is this:

Truth from many sources

I read books, I read science, and I read mythology to find the wisdom that resonates with me.

The sacredness of the individual

My spirituality places the individual above the material, be that economic forces, material objects, or the alleged "word of God."

Radical acceptance

I choose radical acceptance over forcing anyone to conform to my limited and subjective perspective. If a behavior naturally and spontaneously arises (like homosexuality or a transgender identity), it is by definition natural.

The pursuit of a more compassionate society

The real problem is that society is not built to allow for the basic needs of humans beyond earning a paycheck. We need the appropriate social infrastructure to allow humanity to flourish.

The power of one

I am not trying to change the world in a single lifetime; I am planting trees that will grow for future generations.
