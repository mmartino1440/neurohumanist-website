My Philosophy: An Unfolding Journey

My path to neuroscience wasn't a straight line, but a journey fueled by an inquisitive nature I didn't always recognize in myself. It was through the encouragement of teachers over many years that I began to see the potential of my own curiosity. The true catalyst arrived in my final undergraduate semester, in a class on the biology of cardiovascular disease. Learning the intricate details of cellular signaling pathways struck me with a profound sense of awe—that we could understand life at this resolution was a revelation.

This newfound desire to explore biological mechanisms led me to a master's degree, where I became enthralled with every facet of science. I devoured philosophy, the biographies of great thinkers, and even mathematics—a subject I had historically abhorred. As I entered medical school, an itch to explore the brain persisted. When I was finally exposed to the nervous system, I knew I had to commit. It was a risk; I could have fallen back on safer projects in exercise metabolism, but I couldn't pass up the chance to discover if neuroscience and psychiatry were where I could finally unite my eclectic interests in pursuit of a higher objective.

This website is a reflection of that synthesis: a commitment to a science that is not isolated, but is in constant, dynamic conversation with the full breadth of human knowledge.

Research Focus

Core Research: Systems Neuroscience of Fear & Anxiety

As a basic systems neuroscientist in pre-clinical training, my primary work aims to understand how experiences of intense fear become sensitized in the brain. I investigate the whole-brain signature of fear sensitization at both regional and network scales. By combining cutting-edge neuroimaging techniques (like whole-brain c-Fos mapping with iDISCO and light sheet microscopy) with targeted chemogenetic manipulations in my behavioral paradigms, I seek to identify the distributed circuits that mediate the transition from adaptive fear to maladaptive, generalized anxiety.

The Bookshelf

The books that have shaped my thinking. Here are my reflections on texts that bridge science, philosophy, and the human condition.

Parenting & Development

Good Inside

by Dr. Becky Kennedy

Key Takeaway:

All children (and adults) are good inside. This principle reframes challenging behaviors not as malice, but as signals of unmet needs, empowering parents to connect with their children's underlying emotions before correcting behavior.

Best For:

Parents seeking a compassionate, effective, and less shame-based approach to raising resilient and emotionally healthy children.

Rest, Play, Grow

by Deborah MacNamara

Key Takeaway:

True emotional maturation for children doesn't come from teaching, but from the safety of a strong attachment to caregivers. This relationship provides the essential ground for rest, free play, and the emergence of a resilient self.

Best For:

Parents, educators, and therapists interested in an attachment-based developmental approach that explains the deep roots of a child's emotional world.

Behavior & Society

Behave

by Robert Sapolsky

Key Takeaway:

To understand any human behavior, you must examine it at multiple timescales simultaneously: from the neurochemistry of the preceding second, to the hormonal environment of the preceding hours, to the evolutionary pressures of the preceding millennia.

Best For:

Anyone seeking a deep, integrative, and profoundly scientific understanding of why humans do the things they do. A magnum opus of behavioral science.

The Better Angels of Our Nature

by Steven Pinker

Key Takeaway:

Contrary to popular belief, violence has been in decline for centuries. Pinker marshals vast amounts of data to argue that forces like empathy, reason, commerce, and effective government have made the world more peaceful than ever.

Best For:

Those interested in history, sociology, and data-driven arguments about human progress. A powerful counter-narrative to the idea of inevitable decline.

Knowledge & Work

Deep Work

by Cal Newport

Key Takeaway:

The ability to focus without distraction on a cognitively demanding task is a rare and valuable skill. Cultivating a "deep work" ethic is essential for producing high-quality work and finding fulfillment in a world full of shallow distractions.

Best For:

Students, academics, and professionals in any field who feel their attention is constantly fragmented. A practical guide to reclaiming focus.

Mastery

by Robert Greene

Key Takeaway:

True mastery is not about talent but about a long, dedicated apprenticeship. It requires finding your life's task, learning from masters, and pushing through creative plateaus with immense patience.

Best For:

Those graduating high school or college. An essential roadmap for thinking about a long-term career and life path with purpose and intention.

The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat

by Oliver Sacks

Key Takeaway:

Neurological disorders reveal the brain's fragility but also its incredible capacity for adaptation. The "self" is not a single entity but a fragile construction of memory, perception, and narrative.

Best For:

Aspiring clinicians and neuroscientists. It teaches the profound importance of seeing the patient's humanity beyond the diagnosis. A masterclass in compassionate, narrative medicine.

Slow Productivity

by Cal Newport

Key Takeaway:

An antidote to modern burnout culture. The path to impactful work is not through frantic, high-volume effort, but by working at a natural pace, reducing overhead, and obsessing over quality.

Best For:

Knowledge workers, academics, and creatives feeling overwhelmed by the pressure to be constantly "on" and productive. A philosophy for a more sustainable and meaningful career.

AI as a Research Partner

Several colleagues have expressed interest in how I use AI to develop ideas and visualizations. My approach is rooted in partnership: I see AI not as a mere tool, but as a collaborator in the scientific and creative process. Here’s a look into our workflow.

Ideation & Synthesis

The greatest challenge for a neuro-humanist is connecting disparate fields. I use AI as a Socratic partner to bridge these gaps. By posing high-level questions, I can rapidly explore potential links between, for instance, 19th-century literature on ennui and modern neurobiological models of the default mode network. The AI acts as an intellectual catalyst, helping to synthesize vast amounts of information and suggest novel lines of inquiry that I can then pursue with academic rigor.

Rapid Prototyping & Visualization

Compelling ideas deserve compelling presentation. I co-develop visualizations, user interfaces, and even entire websites (like this one) with AI. I provide the architectural vision, the scientific content, and the aesthetic direction, while the AI handles the rapid generation of code. This iterative dialogue allows me to translate complex concepts into accessible, interactive formats in a fraction of the time it would take alone.

An Open Philosophy

I am committed to the principles of open science. I believe that knowledge, including the methods used to generate it, should not be gatekept. This section, and the insights within it, will always be freely accessible. For those who are interested in supporting this work and getting a deeper, behind-the-scenes look at my projects, I may explore community-funding models like Patreon in the future. The goal is to build a community, not a paywall.

Horizons: Exploring New Frontiers

Science advances when we look beyond established boundaries. These emerging fields represent exciting future directions for a truly integrated science of the mind.

Neuroaesthetics

What are the neural underpinnings of beauty, art, and the sublime? This field investigates how our brains process aesthetic experiences, offering a bridge between art history, creativity, and circuit-level neuroscience.

Neurolaw

How should our understanding of the brain impact the justice system? Neurolaw grapples with complex questions of free will, culpability, and memory, aiming to inform a more neurobiologically-grounded legal framework.

Contemplative Science

By studying the minds of expert meditators, we can map the neural basis of well-being, compassion, and attention. This field provides a powerful, proactive approach to understanding mental health, not just mental illness.

Neuroarchitecture

How do the spaces we inhabit shape our brains and behavior? This fascinating field explores the connection between the built environment and our cognitive and emotional states, with implications for designing healthier cities and buildings.
